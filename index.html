Bio

Ph.D. Student in Economics, University of Pittsburgh

I am a Ph.D. student in Economics at the University of Pittsburgh. My work lies at the intersection of decision theory, econometric theory, and optimization, with a focus on axiomatic foundations of choice, stochastic choice and incomplete preferences, and the econometrics of partially identified and high-dimensional models.

Pittsburgh, PA, USA
behroozmoosavi@pitt.edu
+1 (412) 459-6774

Much of my research is motivated by bringing tools from variational analysis, random set theory, and optimization into applied and theoretical econometrics. I am particularly interested in how these tools can sharpen partial identification, improve variable selection in unstable environments, and inform algorithms for sampling from complex, nonconvex distributions.

Before coming to Pitt, I completed a B.Sc. in Mathematical Sciences (Cum Laude) at Sharif University of Technology in Tehran, where I built a strong foundation in real analysis, topology, algebra, and probability. I have also developed lecture notes and course materials on topics such as topology, linear algebra, and mathematical logic.

Outside research, I enjoy teaching and mentoring students, especially in courses that bridge rigorous mathematics and economic applications.

Research

Decision Theory, Econometric Theory, and Optimization

I study decision-making under uncertainty and econometric models where parameters are partially identified or live in high-dimensional spaces. My work combines tools from stochastic choice theory, random set theory, and modern optimization.
